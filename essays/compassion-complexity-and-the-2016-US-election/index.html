Compassion, Complexity, and the 2016 U.S. Election

A look into America's approach at modern day politics through the lens of social media.

The United States election is exactly two days from the time I am typing this out. It seems like it's been a year that the only news that has crossed my eyes has been related to the presidential candidates. In the timespan though things have happened all over the world from the 70 year reign of the king of Thailand coming to an end, to Rome electing its first female mayor, and who can forget Britain's decision to leave the European Union. The two main party candidates will not be discussed in this discourse as there's been enough talk of them for a lifetime. What this will explore is the constant dismissal and belittling of others opinions that has been rampant in this election and why it is a poignantly and frighteningly un-American aspect of this election.

If you spend any amount of time on the internet and you will see the constant barrage of people calling others who oppose their candidate ignorant, evil, and anti-american. This occurs in every election, because many people have the tendency to believe that they are standing on the correct side of an issue or a decision. If people thought they were wrong then they would flip to the other side of the issue and then they would think they are correct. In the end what this comes down to is that one side or person thinks that they understand the issue at a more fundamental level than an opponent. The important thing to remember is that there is rarely an argument made that has no footing or rationale behind it at all, and the even more important thing to remember is that for some problems there isn't one best way to do everything.

The reality is, for a large number of policies it is likely to help some group and be detrimental to another group. This isn't always the case but it's certainly the trend, especially when it comes to economic policies. In a time like this I think the most dangerous thing that the citizens of this country can do is to not recognize the complexity that is behind these issues. We need to take the responsibility of recognizing that the answers aren't clear cut and one proposed solution doesn't necessarily work for everybody. When somebody is accusing another of being ignorant and just dismissing their opinion it leads to a lack of attempted empathy for another individual. If there's any thing that we should do to build a stronger community it's to listen to each other. We need to take responsibility for gaining a fuller understanding of how all citizens in our society live and to attempt to understand why they believe the things they do. Only this will help us craft better policies, strive toward equality, and become more compassionate individuals.
